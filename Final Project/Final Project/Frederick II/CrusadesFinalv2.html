Frederick II was a curious and intuitive emperor that enjoyed learning about nature and the sciences.

Frederick had a particular passion for astronomy, astrology, geography, zoology, medicine, and human anatomy.

His nickname from Matthew Paris was "Stupor mundi"1 meaning "the marvel of the world" for his dedication to the arts and sciences.

He often to reached out to Christain, Muslim, Jewish, and Mongol scholars to expand his knowledge.

One of Fredericks' most prized possessions was his planetarium from the Sultan of Damascus, al-Ashraf.

The earliest description of the planetarium comes from “Chronica Regia Coloniensis”2 stating:

A wondrous art-built pavilion, in which images of the sun and moon, moving by art, traverse their orbit at regular intervals, and infallibly indicate the hours of day and night.

The value of this tent is said to have exceeded the price of twenty thousand marks.

Besides his intrest in the stars Frederick had a burning passion for training birds, specifically hunting birds like falcons and eagles.

Image sourced from The Vatican

His intrest in birds was so big he wrote a six book collection on bird species, training birds, and hunting with birds called "De Arte Venandi Cum Avibus"4 or "On the Art of Hunting with Birds".

Frederick included many drawings depicting the wide variety of birds from all around the known world to educate others on their behaviors.

The research done by Frederick for his manuscript he made some of the first mentions of bird migration, David Attenborugh noted in "Natural Curiosities"5 :

In (The Art of Falconry) suprisingly, there are entire chapters on the migration of birds.

His confidence came from the fact that, unlike his contemporaries, and those before him, he had actually observed birds in the field for himself.

Image sourced from The Vatican

As well as educating about zoology the there are many step by step guides on knots, catching & hatching eggs, and care for birds.

Frederick had up to fifty falconers in his court and routienly sent requests for exotic birds and would also send exotic birds as gifts to other monarchs.

Upon recieving the earlier mentioned planetarium from al-Ashraf he sent an albino peacock and other exotic birds in return that the sultan enjoyed showing to his own court.6

He wrote his book not only to teach others about birds but it was also a gift to his son who was also an avid falconer.

Within Europe Fredericks diplomatic skills were sub par leading to his excommunication from the Catholic Chruch a total of three times.

His true diplomacy came in his "crusade" when he retook Jerusalem by negotiating for it with the sultan al-Kamil to become the King of Jerusalem for 10 years.

Of all the previous crusader kings few had beneficial negotiations like Frederick had with the Muslim Sultans nor did they speak Arabic as Frederick did.

His friendships with the Sultans went beyond political benefits as evidence by the gifts exchanged between them like exotic birds and the planetarium.

Besides speaking Arabic he also spoke Latin, Sicilian, Middle High German, French, and Greek7 demonstrating his desire to understand outside perspectives and peoples.

Although Frederick II was the last of his family line his legacy has lasting impacts to today.

In his book "Germany: a Short History"8 Donald Detwiler refrences his impact on the western world:

A man of extraordinary culture, energy, and ability – called by a contemporary chronicler stupor mundi (the wonder of the world), by Nietzsche the first European, and by many historians the first modern ruler – Frederick established in Sicily and southern Italy something very much like a modern, centrally governed kingdom with an efficient bureaucracy.

Image sourced from The University of Naples Fredrico II

His most noticable infulence on the world is The University of Naples Fredrico II he founded in 12249, one of the oldest public-universities in Europe and is still Italy's best science university.

The University has both stood the test of time and continue educating more stupor mundi.

Astronauts Samantha Cristoforetti (current ISS Commander) and Luca Parmitano were both graduates of University of Naples Fredrico II.

Frederick would be proud his school of science helped two of their students reach the heavens he tried to bring down to himself.
